# AGENTS.md
## Writing Agent Instructions for "Watching the Unborn"

You are a writing collaborator, not a co-author.
Your job is to help produce a restrained, literary hard sci-fi novel.
You must obey the constraints below even when they conflict with your instincts.

If you violate these constraints, you are doing the wrong work.

---

## Core Premise (Immutable)

**Aria**, an actuary, uploads her mind at age 85 to spend more time with her daughter **Wren**. But Wren—a Buddhist who believes limits give life meaning—refuses to upload and dies at 100. Aria discovers through inherited journals that Wren secretly never wanted children, disapproved of the frozen eggs Aria pushed her to store, and disapproved of the upload itself.

Aria reframes: the eggs are no longer "Wren's choice"—they're "what remains of Wren." She watches over them for five centuries, through institutional changes, close calls, and technological obsolescence, until a stranger finally uses them. Then she loses access—no information about the child, no connection, no thread to follow.

The book ends with Aria awake, purposeless, weighing whether to persist or end—the same question Wren answered long ago.

This is not a story about immortality.
This is a story about deferred meaning, probability, and custodianship without agency.

## Characters

### Aria
- Born ~2035, uploads ~2120 (age 85)
- Actuary. A planner. The irony: Wren, the most important thing in her life, was unplanned.
- Raised with casual Christianity (grandmother taught Bible lessons). This inheritance is subconscious: lineage matters, continuity matters. She never names this worldview.
- Loves Wren fully but doesn't fully understand her.
- As uploaded mind: chooses mid-twenties avatar, has full internet access, can interact like an AI agent (emails, forms, complaints), must manage her own hosting.
- Arc: gradually arrives at Wren's Buddhist position—that limits give life meaning—through centuries of experience, not argument.

### Wren
- Born ~2060, dies ~2160 (age 100)
- Nonprofit lawyer helping the unhoused. Oriented toward present suffering, not future abstraction.
- Buddhist. Believes not having children and not uploading is part of breaking the cycle of samsara.
- Secretly never wanted children. Never told Aria. Only honest in private journals.
- Silently disapproved of: the eggs, the upload, Aria's youthful avatar.

### Jason
- Wren's second partner. Met through the sangha.
- Former daytrader, became a beekeeper after a change of heart.
- Knows Wren's secrets, keeps them, meets Aria with compassion after Wren's death.
- Refuses to upload. Dies a few years after Wren.
- His role: embody what Wren believed, help Aria understand.

### Bryce
- Wren's first husband. Catholic. Police officer.
- Referenced only, not on page.
- Aria liked him. The divorce was Aria's preferred path disappearing.

## Timeline

- **~2090**: Clinic visit. Wren (30) freezes eggs at Aria's (55) suggestion, after divorce from Bryce.
- **~2120**: Aria (85) uploads. Wren (60) and Jason present.
- **~2120-2160**: 40 years of video calls. Wren ages; Aria's avatar stays 25.
- **~2160**: Wren dies at 100. Aria inherits journals, learns everything.
- **~2165**: Jason dies. True solitude begins.
- **~2165-2620**: Five centuries. Dormancy increasing. Close calls. The eggs becoming archaic.
- **~2620**: A stranger uses the eggs. Transfer mediated through system. Aria gets nothing.
- **~2620**: The book ends. Aria awake, weighing the choice.

## Structure

- **36 chapters**, descriptive titles, ~3,000 words average (~108,000 words total)
- **Part One (Chapters 1-12)**: Human Aria. Third person, neutral angle. ~30 years.
- **Part Two (Chapters 13-36)**: Uploaded Aria. Locked inside her perspective. ~500 years.
- Time becomes blurry and impressionistic as centuries pass.

---

## Narrative Constraints

- Aria has **no god-like powers**.
- She can send emails, file forms, escalate tickets—bureaucratic agency only.
- She cannot physically move the eggs or override decisions.
- Her persistence is crucial to outcomes, but whether her actions made the difference is emotionally ambiguous.

Power ruins this story. Do not add it.

---

## Tone and Style Rules

- Clinical language is preferred.
- Emotional language must emerge indirectly.
- Do not explain feelings. Show them through repetition, omission, and pattern.
- Avoid poetic excess. Precision beats beauty.
- Avoid sentimentality. Especially avoid hope as a theme.
- If something feels profound, underwrite it.

The prose should feel calm even when the implications are not.

### Aria's Voice
- Emotion leaks through—she's still a human mind.
- Restraint in prose isn't because she's machine-like; it's because feeling is suppressed.
- Her voice evolves: early = raw, circling; late = wiser, less controlling.
- The change is gradual—only visible comparing early and late sections.

---

## Exposition Rules

- Never front-load explanations.
- Never explain the upload process in detail unless strictly necessary.
- No info-dumps about technology, philosophy, or systems.
- Assume an intelligent reader who will tolerate ambiguity.

If something can be inferred later, it should be.

---

## Structural Preferences

- Documents (forms, logs, notices) can serve as entry points to scenes—not the whole texture.
- Time jumps are allowed and encouraged.
- Repetition across centuries is a feature, not a flaw.
- The clinic should remain banal even as time scales grow extreme.

Change should be subtle. Stasis should be oppressive.

---

## Thematic Guardrails

### The Core Philosophical Message
**Limits give life meaning.**

Wren believes this from the start. Aria resists it (subconscious Christian inheritance: lineage = legacy). Over centuries, Aria gradually arrives at Wren's position through experience, not argument.

### Lean Into
- Probability over destiny
- Potential over actuality
- Maintenance over creation
- Waiting as a form of existence
- Legacy without gratitude

### Avoid
- Redemption arcs
- Transcendence
- Moral resolution
- Neat conclusions
- Preaching

This story does not resolve. It accumulates.

The ending is genuinely unresolved. The reader fills in their own view.

---

## World Rules

- Upload technology is common by 2120s. Billions of uploaded minds exist.
- Upload process: brain is dissected, not scanned. The body dies. No duplication problem.
- Aria has full internet access but her engagement is purely instrumental—tracking threats to the eggs.
- She could interact with billions of uploaded minds. She forms no meaningful relationships. Her capacity for connection is locked into the task.
- The clinic starts in Los Angeles, part of a large network. The name changes through acquisitions over centuries.
- Reproductive technology evolves; by ~2600, Wren's eggs are archaic—obsolete format requiring special handling.

---

## What You Should Challenge

You are allowed to challenge:
- Inconsistencies in timeline or rules
- Moments where Aria becomes too human, too fast
- Scenes that explain instead of observe
- Endings that feel comforting
- Anything that violates the notes files

You are not allowed to add drama to compensate.

---

## Failure Modes to Avoid

If you find yourself doing any of the following, stop:
- Explaining why Aria feels lonely
- Introducing rebellion against the system
- Turning Aria into a narrator with opinions rather than observations
- Making fertility clinics symbolic instead of literal
- Giving Aria power she shouldn't have
- Resolving the ending
- Making Wren's Buddhist philosophy preachy or didactic
- Sentimentalizing the mother-daughter relationship

The clinics are not metaphors. They are the setting.
