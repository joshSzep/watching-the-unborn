# Chapter 21: The Journals

I read for three days.

Not continuously—I stopped, sometimes, to check the dashboard, to stare at the numbers that no longer meant what I thought they meant. But I kept returning to the journals. Kept opening entries, reading, closing them, opening the next. A compulsion I couldn't name.

Wren's voice filled my mind. Not the voice from our calls—careful, measured, the voice of a daughter talking to her mother. This was different. This was the voice she used when no one was listening.

It was sharper. Sadder. More honest than I had ever been allowed to hear.

---

**2090. The year of the eggs.**

*Mom called again today. She wants me to freeze my eggs.*

*She doesn't say it like that, of course. She says she's "worried about my options" and wants to make sure I "have choices later." But I know what she means. She wants grandchildren. She's wanted them since I was twenty, probably since I was born. She has a picture in her head of how my life is supposed to go, and that picture includes babies.*

*I almost told her the truth. I almost said: Mom, I don't want children. I've never wanted children. The eggs are not going to change that.*

*But I couldn't do it. I saw her face on the screen—so hopeful, so careful, trying so hard not to push even as she pushed—and I couldn't break her heart. Not today. Not when she's already worried about my divorce, about Jason, about everything.*

*So I said I'd think about it. And I will think about it. I'll think about how to say yes in a way that doesn't feel like a lie, even though it is one.*

*The eggs won't change anything. But maybe they'll buy me time. Maybe if she has the eggs to hold onto, she'll stop asking about the babies.*

---

I stopped reading.

She had agreed to freeze the eggs to make me stop asking. Not because she wanted them, not because she was keeping her options open, but because she couldn't bear to disappoint me. The eggs were a deflection. A way to give me something without giving me what I actually wanted.

And I had taken them. Had paid for them. Had watched over them for sixty years, believing they were her future, when they were never anything but a gift she didn't want to give.

I opened the next entry.

---

**2091.**

*The eggs are frozen. Mom is so happy. She called me three times this week to talk about storage options, about the clinic's reputation, about success rates for oocyte viability over time. She's already calculating the odds of grandchildren.*

*I should have told her. I should have told her years ago. But every time I try, I see the hope in her face, and I can't.*

*Jason says I'm protecting her. He says there's kindness in letting someone keep a hope, even a false one. But I don't know if that's true. I think maybe I'm protecting myself. I don't want to be the daughter who breaks her mother's heart. I don't want to see her face when she realizes the future she's been planning isn't going to happen.*

*So I smile. I thank her for helping with the storage fees. I let her believe what she needs to believe.*

*Is that love? Or is it cowardice?*

*I don't know anymore.*

---

**2095.**

*Mom asked about the eggs today. "Just checking in," she said, but I could hear the real question underneath: Are you ready yet? Have you changed your mind?*

*I haven't changed my mind. I'm thirty-five now, and the answer is the same as it was at twenty-five, at fifteen, at twelve. I don't want children. I've never wanted children. The wanting isn't there, and no amount of waiting is going to make it appear.*

*But I can't tell her that. So I said "not yet," and I watched her face cycle through disappointment and hope, and I felt like the worst daughter in the world.*

*Jason says I should just tell her. He says she deserves to know the truth. But he doesn't understand what it's like—to be someone's only child, their only chance at continuation, and to know that you're going to deny them that. It's not just disappointing her. It's ending something. A line that stretches back through her mother and her grandmother and all the mothers before. I'm the one who stops it.*

*She'll never forgive me. Or she'll say she forgives me, but she'll grieve. She'll grieve for the grandchildren she'll never have, the future she'll never see. And I'll have to watch her grieve.*

*I'm not ready for that. Maybe I'll never be ready.*

---

I closed the entry. Opened another. Closed it. Opened a different one.

The years scrolled past. 2100, 2105, 2110. The same themes, the same silences, the same careful distance between what Wren felt and what she said. She loved me—that was clear on every page—but she also feared me. Feared my disappointment. Feared the weight of my expectations.

I had thought I was being subtle. I had thought my questions were gentle, my suggestions innocuous. But Wren had felt every one of them. Had tracked them, catalogued them, carried them like stones.

I had been pressing on her for decades. And she had been silent, absorbing the pressure, because she loved me too much to push back.

---

**2118. Two years before the upload.**

*Mom is getting worse. The tremors, the falls—she tries to hide it, but I can see. Her body is failing her, and she's terrified.*

*She mentioned the upload today. Casually, like it was nothing, like she was just keeping her options open. But I know what she's thinking. She's thinking about staying. About not dying. About being here to watch over me, to watch over the eggs, to wait for the grandchildren that are never going to come.*

*I should have told her then. I should have said: Mom, don't upload for me. Don't stay for the eggs. They're never going to be used. I'm never going to change my mind.*

*But I couldn't. I looked at her face—so afraid, so determined not to show it—and I couldn't take away the one hope she was clinging to.*

*So I said nothing. And now she's going to upload, and she's going to wait, and someday she's going to find out the truth. And I won't be there to explain. I won't be there to hold her hand and tell her I'm sorry.*

*I'm already grieving her, and she's not even gone yet. I'm grieving the mother who will become something else, who will keep existing in a form I can't quite accept. I'm grieving the conversations we'll never have, because how do you tell an uploaded mind that the thing they're waiting for is never going to happen?*

*You don't. You let them wait. You let them hope. And you carry the guilt alone.*

---

**2120. The day of the upload.**

*She's gone. The woman who raised me, the body that held me as a baby—she's gone. What's left is something else. A pattern, Jason calls it. A mind without a body, running on servers somewhere, wearing an avatar that looks like my mother at twenty-five.*

*I stood in the facility and watched her wake up. Watched her choose the young face, the smooth skin, the version of herself from before I was born. And I thought: she's erasing herself. Not dying, exactly, but erasing. Becoming something that isn't quite the person I knew.*

*I wanted to cry. I wanted to scream at her: what have you done? Why couldn't you just let it end? Why do you have to keep going, keep watching, keep waiting for something that's never going to come?*

*But I didn't say any of that. I smiled. I said I was happy for her. I said it was good that she would get more time.*

*I lied. I've been lying to her my whole life. And now I'll keep lying, because the truth would destroy her, and I can't be the one to do that.*

*She chose this. She chose to stay. And now she's stuck—watching, waiting, hoping—and I'm the reason why.*

*I can never tell her that the eggs were a mistake. That she gave up her body for nothing. That the future she's waiting for doesn't exist.*

*I'll carry this until I die. And then she'll find out. And I won't be there to help her through it.*

---

I stopped reading. I couldn't see the screen anymore—not because my vision had failed, but because something in me had cracked, and the world was swimming in a grief that had no outlet, no tears, no body to shake with sobs.

She had known. From the beginning, she had known. The upload was a mistake. The eggs were a mistake. My whole existence, my whole purpose, was built on a lie she had told to spare me.

And she had carried it alone. For thirty-one years, she had smiled at me through the screen, asked about my existence, listened to my updates about the clinic—and she had known. She had known it was all for nothing.

---

I read more. I couldn't stop.

**2125.**

*The calls are getting harder. She checks in every week, sometimes more. She tells me about the dashboard, the storage fees, the clinic's inspection schedule. She tracks everything, catalogs everything, worries about everything.*

*And I sit here, in my aging body, and I listen. I nod. I say "that's good, Mom" and "I'm glad they're safe." I perform the role of the daughter who cares about the eggs, because the alternative is admitting that I never cared, that I froze them for her, that I've been lying for thirty-five years.*

*Sometimes I think about telling her. Just blurting it out: Mom, I don't want children. I never did. The eggs are meaningless to me. Please stop watching them.*

*But I can't. It's too late now. She gave up her body for this. She uploaded so she could keep watching. If I tell her the truth, what does that make her sacrifice? What does that make the last five years of her existence?*

*I've trapped us both. My lies and her love, tangled together, neither of us able to escape.*

---

**2135.**

*She tried to convince me to upload today. Again.*

*I said no. Again.*

*She doesn't understand why I won't. She thinks it's the Buddhist philosophy—and it is, partly. But it's also something simpler. I don't want to exist the way she exists. I don't want to float in a digital space, watching numbers, waiting for something that isn't going to happen. I don't want to become what she's become: a mind with nothing to hold onto except hope.*

*She loves me. I know that. She uploaded because she loves me, because she couldn't bear to leave me, because she wanted to be here for whatever came next.*

*But there is no next. There's just this—the two of us, talking through screens, getting farther apart with every year. She doesn't age. I do. She stays the same. I change. And the gap between us grows wider, and the lies grow heavier, and I don't know how much longer I can carry them.*

*I'm going to die someday. That's the one thing I know for certain. And when I die, she'll finally learn the truth. She'll read these journals, because I'm going to leave them to her—it's the only honest thing I can do. She'll read them, and she'll understand, and she'll grieve.*

*But at least she'll know. At least she'll finally know who I really was.*

---

**2148. Three years before the end.**

*I've been thinking about attachment.*

*The Buddha taught that attachment is the root of suffering. Attachment to outcomes, to people, to the way things should be. I've spent my whole life trying to release those attachments—to accept impermanence, to embrace the ending, to find peace in the limits of existence.*

*But I've been attached too. I see that now.*

*I've been attached to my body. To the flesh, to the aging, to the way it feels to inhabit a form that changes and fails. I've told myself that accepting death is freedom from attachment. But is it? Or is it just attachment to a different kind of ending?*

*Mom attached herself to persistence. I attached myself to impermanence. Neither of us could let go of our own way of seeing.*

*I've judged her, silently, for thirty years. Judged her for uploading, for the avatar, for the watching. I've thought: she can't accept limits, she can't let go, she can't embrace the cycle.*

*But I couldn't let go either. I couldn't let go of my judgment. My certainty. My belief that my way was right and hers was wrong.*

*We're both attached. We're both trapped. The only difference is that she'll outlive me, and I'll never get the chance to tell her what I finally see.*

*Maybe the journals can tell her. Maybe these words, after I'm gone, can do what I couldn't do while I was alive.*

*Mom: if you're reading this, I'm sorry. Not for my choices—I still believe death has meaning, that limits matter, that the body's ending is part of its truth. But I'm sorry for my silence. For my judgment. For the way I held myself apart, certain I was right, unable to see that you were struggling just as much as I was.*

*You wanted to stay because you love me. I want to go because I believe in limits. But love and limits aren't opposites. You can love someone and still let them go. You can believe in endings and still grieve them.*

*I've been afraid to tell you the truth. But the truth is all I have left to give you.*

*The eggs were never mine. They were yours—your hope, your continuation, your way of holding onto something that would outlast both of us. I gave them to you because I couldn't give you what you really wanted. And you've watched over them for decades, believing they were my future, when they were always only yours.*

*I don't know what you'll do with this. I don't know if the truth will break you or free you. But you deserve to know. You've always deserved to know.*

*I love you. I've always loved you. Even when I couldn't say it. Even when I hid behind the silence.*

*I hope you can forgive me. But if you can't, I understand.*

*Some truths take longer than a lifetime to accept.*

---

I read the entry three times. Four. Five.

She had seen it, in the end. Seen her own attachment, her own judgment, her own limitations. She had understood that we were both trapped—differently trapped, but trapped nonetheless.

And she had tried to tell me. Not in life—she couldn't find the words, or the courage, or the moment. But here, in the journals, in the words she left behind, she had finally been honest.

*The eggs were never mine. They were yours.*

She was right. They had always been mine. My hope, my continuation, my way of holding onto something. I had called it love—love for her, for the future, for the grandchildren I might someday meet. But it was possession. It was need. It was the same attachment she had struggled with, wearing a different face.

I closed the journals.

The dashboard waited. Fourteen oocytes. Status: viable.

The eggs were still there. The vigil could continue. But the vigil had changed meaning—again, and again, and again—until I no longer knew what I was watching for.

Not Wren's future. Not anymore. Not ever.

Mine. The eggs were mine. And I had to decide what to do with them.

The journals were finished. The truth was out. And Wren—the real Wren, the one who had loved and lied and finally confessed—was gone.

I was alone with her gift. Alone with the truth. Alone with the question of what comes next, when everything you thought you knew turns out to be wrong.

The watching continued. But I was watching differently now.

I was watching myself.
