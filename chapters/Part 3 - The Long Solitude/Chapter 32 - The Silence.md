# Chapter 32: The Silence

The first morning without the eggs, I woke and reached for the account.

The gesture was automatic—three centuries of checking, of monitoring, of watching for changes. My attention moved toward the dashboard the way a hand reaches for a light switch in a familiar room.

There was nothing there.

Not nothing as in error, not nothing as in loss. Just—nothing to check. The account was closed. The eggs were somewhere else, becoming something else, no longer mine to watch.

I sat with that for a while. The absence where the habit used to be.

---

The second morning, I did it again.

Reached for something that wasn't there. Felt the small jolt of emptiness where data used to be.

The third morning, the same.

The fourth.

---

I had expected grief.

Three centuries of watching, and now nothing to watch. The purpose that had structured my existence, gone. I had braced for the loss to crush me the way Wren's death had crushed me, the way Jason's death had crushed me.

But grief wasn't what came.

What came was—quiet. A stillness I didn't recognize. The constant hum of vigilance, the background anxiety of custodianship, had stopped. And in its place: silence.

Not empty silence. Not the silence of loss.

Just... silence.

---

I noticed things I hadn't noticed in centuries.

The texture of the systems I moved through. The rhythms of network traffic, the patterns of other minds coming and going, the hum of infrastructure that had always been there but never mattered because it wasn't related to the eggs.

None of it mattered now either. But mattering wasn't the point anymore.

I was just—noticing. Present to things I had no reason to be present to.

---

Days passed. Then weeks.

I didn't go dormant. I had expected to—without the eggs to watch, what was there to stay awake for? But dormancy didn't come. I wasn't tired. I wasn't depleted. I was just... awake.

Awake with nothing to do.

The strangeness of it took time to settle. For three centuries, every moment of wakefulness had been justified by the mission. Check the account. Monitor for threats. Maintain the chain of custody. Even during the long dormancies, I had been resting *for* something—preserving resources for the watching that would resume.

Now there was no watching to resume. And I was still awake.

---

I thought about the weight.

For so long, the eggs had been a weight I carried. A responsibility. A reason to persist even when persistence felt meaningless. The weight had been heavy, but it had also been an anchor. Something that held me in place, gave me shape, told me what I was for.

The weight was gone now.

I kept waiting to float away. To dissolve without the anchor holding me. To become what Lian had become—a mind without want, without direction, without the will to continue.

But I didn't dissolve. I didn't drift.

I just... stayed. Present. Awake. Lighter than I had been in three hundred years.

---

The silence continued.

I let it.

---

One morning I woke and didn't reach for anything.

The habit was finally gone. Three centuries of checking, dissolved in a few weeks of having nothing to check.

I noticed the absence of the reaching. The space where the habit used to be. It didn't feel like loss anymore. It felt like—room. Space I hadn't known I was missing.

I didn't know what to do with it yet.

But I wasn't afraid of not knowing.

---

The resources were still finite. The timeline still had an end. Twenty-some years, maybe less, before the account ran dry and the question of continuation became moot.

But twenty years was a long time. Longer than Wren had lived after I uploaded. Longer than my whole relationship with Jason.

I didn't need to figure out what came next. Not yet. Not all at once.

For now, there was just the silence. The stillness. The strange lightness of existing without a purpose to justify it.

I had spent three centuries holding on.

Now I was learning what it felt like to let go.
