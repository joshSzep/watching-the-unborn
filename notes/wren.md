# Wren

## Basic Information
- **Born:** ~2060
- **Died:** ~2160 (age 100, natural causes)
- **Profession:** Lawyer at a nonprofit working to help the unhoused

## Personality
- The opposite of Aria: responds to what happens, doesn't plan.
- Oriented toward present suffering, not future abstraction.
- Already practicing a kind of engaged compassion before Buddhism gave it a name.

## Key Relationships
- **Aria:** Her mother. Warm relationship, but the warmth comes from friction. She loves her mother but doesn't share her worldview. Keeps secrets from her out of love or avoidance.
- **Bryce:** First husband. Catholic. Police officer. The marriage ended when Wren met Jason.
- **Jason:** Second partner. Buddhist. Met through her sangha. The relationship that mattered.

## The Eggs
- Aria's idea. Wren agreed after the divorce, during a vulnerable moment.
- She already knew she didn't want children.
- She never tells Aria. Keeps this secret her entire life.
- The only place she's honest is in private writing she believes will stay private.

## Buddhism
- Found it herself, probably after the divorce.
- Meets Jason through her sangha.
- Believes strongly that life being limited is essential to life having meaning.
- Believes not having children and not uploading is part of breaking the cycle of samsara.
- The eggs and the upload are the same refusal to her—both perpetuate a cycle she's trying to end.

## Hidden Disapprovals
Wren silently disapproves of:
1. The eggs (she doesn't want children)
2. Aria's upload (goes against her Buddhist values)
3. Aria's youthful avatar (another form of denial)

She never voices any of this. Aria learns it all from the journals after Wren's death.

## Relationship to Death
- Wren watches Aria upload, knowing she disapproves, saying nothing.
- When Aria later tries to convince Wren to upload, Wren refuses.
- She gives her reason: limits give life meaning.
- She dies of natural old age at 100.
- She doesn't consider that Aria will inherit and read her private journals.

## The Journals
- Wren's private writing is the only place she's honest about her true views.
- She never imagined her mother reading them. They were a refuge, not a message.
- After her death, they pass to Aria as part of the estate—a legal default Wren never thought to change.
