# Aria

## Basic Information
- **Born:** ~2035
- **Upload:** ~2120 (age 85)
- **Profession:** Actuary

## Personality
- A planner. Makes plans, doesn't respond to events.
- The irony: the most important thing in her life (Wren) was unplanned.
- This may be why she overcompensates—trying to secure futures, control outcomes.
- Loves Wren fully but doesn't fully understand her.
- Naturally warms to people who make Wren happy, even when she doesn't understand them.

## Background
- Grew up with Christian parents who didn't push it—no church, not serious about it.
- Grandmother was strongly religious, taught her Bible lessons as a child.
- This inheritance is subconscious: lineage matters, continuity matters, children are legacy.
- She never names this worldview. It's assumed, not argued.
- Agnostic. Not Buddhist, not atheist.

## Key Relationships
- **Wren:** Her daughter. Unplanned. Wren's father died when Wren was a baby. It's always been the two of them. Warm relationship, but the warmth comes from friction—like rubbing hands together to keep from being cold.
- **Jason:** Wren's partner. Aria warms to him because he makes Wren happy. After Wren dies, Jason becomes Aria's last human connection.
- **Bryce:** Wren's first husband. Aria liked him—saw the marriage as stable, a future she could plan around.

## The Upload Decision
- Her body is failing at 85. Natural old age, not dramatic illness.
- She frames the upload as a way to spend more time with Wren.
- Wren and Jason are both present for the procedure.
- She doesn't know they both silently disapprove.

## As an Uploaded Mind
- **Appearance:** Can appear however she wants. Chooses mid-twenties—the version of herself she liked best.
- **Memory:** Clear. Waking from dormancy is like waking from a power nap, even if it's been 50 years.
- **Perception of time:** Squishy. Early on, she stays awake for years at a stretch. As centuries pass, she goes dormant for longer and longer periods.
- **Access:** Full internet. Can interact with the world like an AI agent—send emails, fill out forms, file complaints, escalate tickets.
- **Constraints:** Can't physically move the eggs. Can't override decisions. Dependent on humans (and later, systems) to do the actual work.
- **Hosting:** She has to manage it herself—fees, migrations, providers. Her own persistence is as bureaucratically precarious as the eggs.

## The Discovery
- After Wren dies, Aria legally inherits her private notes and journals.
- She reads everything at once, with the speed of an uploaded mind.
- She learns: Wren secretly never wanted children. Wren disapproved of the eggs. Wren disapproved of the upload. Wren disapproved of Aria's youthful avatar.
- All of it kept secret out of love, or avoidance, or both.
- Aria stays awake processing this for a long time. Time is squishy.

## Arc Across Centuries
- Early: Raw. Awake for years. Watching eggs that don't change. Circling the same thoughts.
- Middle: The work of persistence. Close calls. Transfers. Forms. Searching for someone to use the eggs.
- Late: Increasingly dormant. Wiser. Less controlling.
- She gradually arrives at Wren's position—that limits give life meaning—through experience, not argument.
- By the end, she understands what Wren understood at the start.

## Relationship to the Eggs
- After learning Wren's secret, she reframes her purpose: the eggs are no longer "Wren's choice"—they're "what remains of Wren."
- This is a conscious reframing. She knows what she's doing.
- Her capacity for connection gets locked into this one task. Forming new relationships would dilute the only purpose she has left.
- She's surrounded by billions of uploaded minds but connects with none of them meaningfully.

## The Search
- She actively searches for a recipient across centuries.
- The eggs become archaic—outdated format, obsolete procedures.
- Finding someone willing to use them takes persistence over hundreds of years.

## The End
- The eggs are finally used by a stranger. Transaction mediated entirely through the system. No direct contact.
- The transfer succeeds. The door shuts. She gets nothing—no information about the child.
- She stays awake. Purposeless.
- She thinks about Wren and Jason. About the choice they made. About the question she's now facing.
- The book ends with her weighing whether to persist or end. The answer is left to the reader.
